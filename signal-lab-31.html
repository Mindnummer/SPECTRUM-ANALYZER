Is a 440 Hz sound the same as a 440 Hz radio wave?
      
No. Frequency is just "cycles per second" — a shared unit. But the carrier is completely different. Sound is mechanical pressure squeezing through a medium (air, water, steel) at the speed of sound; it can't travel through vacuum and carries no photon energy. An electromagnetic wave is oscillating electric and magnetic fields moving at light speed through empty space, and its energy comes in photons of E = h·f. A 440 Hz sound wave and a 440 Hz EM wave share only the number 440 — they are different physical things with wildly different wavelengths (shown above) and energies. The "if it were EM" row is a what-if conversion, not a claim this speaker emits light.

Run a sweep to find resonance peaks.

    

    
Plays a tone sweep through your speaker while the mic listens, then marks where the system (speaker × room × object × mic) responds strongly. This is an uncalibrated relative response — peaks suggest resonances (room modes, rattles, panel buzz), not absolute levels. Needs mic permission in a real browser tab. A peak doesn't identify its source.

Prism
Where color matters. Glass bends bluer wavelengths more than redder ones, fanning white light into a rainbow — refraction, not reflection. The bar spotlights one wavelength. (Schematic, exaggerated.)

      
The Spine — one number, two senses
Left is the color, right is its pitch. Slide the wavelength: the color shifts and the tone climbs together, locked by ÷2⁴⁰ (40 octaves). Tap Play tone to hear the right side while you watch the left. Light itself is never audible — this is an honest pitch-preserving mapping, not a measurement.
